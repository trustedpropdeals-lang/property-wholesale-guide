Property Wholesaling & Arbitrage Guide

Upscale yet practical insights into profiting from real estate without owning property outright.

What is Property Wholesaling?

Property wholesaling is a real estate strategy where an investor puts a property under contract at a below‑market price and then assigns that contract to another buyer for a fee. Instead of buying the property and renovating it, the wholesaler simply secures the rights to purchase and then passes those rights along to a cash buyer. Benefits include:

  • Low capital outlay: You never actually purchase the property, so you avoid large down payments or mortgages.
  • Quick transactions: Deals can close quickly, often within weeks, allowing for rapid profit cycles.
  • Opportunity to help distressed sellers: You often work with owners facing foreclosure or other urgent situations.

Steps to Start Wholesaling

  1. Market research: Study your local real‑estate market, analyze recent comparable sales and understand state regulations.
  2. Build a buyers’ list: Connect with cash buyers (investors) who are ready to purchase deals. Network through real‑estate meetups, online groups and “We Buy Houses” ads.
  3. Find distressed properties: Source under‑valued properties via the MLS, driving for dollars, bandit signs, direct mail or wholesaling software.
  4. Negotiate and secure the contract: Agree on a purchase price with the seller and ensure the contract includes an assignment clause.
  5. Assign the contract: Transfer your purchase agreement to a buyer for an assignment fee or conduct a double closing.
  6. Close through a title company/attorney: Use professionals to ensure all legal requirements are satisfied.

What is Rental Arbitrage?

Rental arbitrage involves leasing a property long‑term and subletting it on short‑term rental platforms (like Airbnb) to capture a higher nightly rate. It allows investors to bypass down payments and mortgage approvals, making real estate investing more accessible.

Steps to Start Rental Arbitrage

  1. Understand local regulations: Confirm that subletting and short‑term rentals are allowed and determine permit, tax and occupancy requirements.
  2. Secure the right property: Choose properties in high‑demand short‑term rental markets with attractive amenities.
  3. Negotiate lease terms: Ensure the lease explicitly permits subletting and short‑term rental use.
  4. Calculate true profit margins: Account for rent, furnishings, utilities, cleaning and platform fees to ensure a positive cash flow.
  5. Launch professionally: Use professional photography, detailed listings and dynamic pricing tools. Provide excellent guest experiences to maximise occupancy and reviews.

Tips and Considerations

  • Legal compliance: Wholesaling is legal in most states but regulations differ; consult a real‑estate attorney for your market.
  • Network building: Cultivate relationships with motivated sellers, cash buyers, landlords and property managers.
  • Education & mentorship: Join investor associations, take webinars and seek experienced mentors.
  • Risk management: Keep emergency funds for vacancies or unexpected repairs and ensure deals have sufficient profit spread.
